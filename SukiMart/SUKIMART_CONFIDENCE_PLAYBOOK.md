## §1 · The case in 90 seconds

**Cristina "Tina" Mahinay**, 44, has run **Tindahan ni Tina** in Barangay Guadalupe for 13 years — the busiest store on her street. About ₱10,000 passes through her hands on a good day. Her rule, for 13 years: *"buy ₱10,000 of goods, sell at 20% more — that 20% is my profit."* It fed her family, sent two kids to school, built **₱600,000 in savings**.

Now a branded 24-hour convenience store opened 1.4km away. Tina wants her own: **SukiMart** — air-conditioned, services, ready-to-eat, *"so my suki finally get the store they deserve."* A cooperative will lend her **₱750,000**; with her ₱600,000 she can build it. She was **ready to sign this week.**

Then her daughter **Jamie** (finishing a business degree) asked the question that stopped her:

> *"Ma. The twenty percent. Once we are paying rent, and aircon, and three staff, and the loan every month — is the twenty percent still enough? Or are we just busier and poorer?"*

Tina has no answer. She has never paid rent (the store is her house), never paid herself a salary, never had a bill due on the 1st whether the store was busy or empty. **That is where we come in.**

**The four questions we must answer (Part 5 of the brief):**

1. **The verdict** — full jump, staged transition, or disciplined sari-sari? Decide, prove it.
2. **The break-even reality** — what sales & mix does SukiMart need to survive, then to profit?
3. **The role of AI** — where does AI earn its place, tied to a real problem, quantified in pesos or risk? *"Buzzwords score nothing."*
4. **The plan** — a 12-month roadmap, a financing/payback view, the three biggest risks + how she manages each.

**Our answer in one line:** *Don't bet the farm — earn the store.* Stage the transition; let the data earn the loan; use AI to kill the one cost that sinks convenience stores — spoilage.

## §4 · Business crashcourse (the 8 ideas that decide the case)

For each: the idea, Tina's number, and the **🎤 on-stage line** to say it cleanly.

**1 · Markup vs. Margin.** Markup measures profit against **cost**; margin against **selling price** — margin is always smaller (`margin = markup ÷ (1+markup)`). Tina's "20% markup" is a **17.5% blended margin** (COGS is 82.5% of sales).
🎤 *"Tina's '20%' is a markup on cost. On the selling price — how you run a P&L — it's a 17.5% margin. Every peso in this case is calculated on 17.5%, not 20%."*

**2 · Fixed vs. Variable cost.** Variable moves with each sale (COGS); fixed arrives monthly regardless (rent, staff, loan). The case's whole trick: a sari-sari **hides** its three biggest fixed costs (rent ₱0 = her house, labor ₱0 = herself, electricity buried in the home bill). **Formalizing turns them into ₱147,000/mo of hard bills.**
🎤 *"A sari-sari hides rent, labor, and electricity. SukiMart turns all three into bills due on the 1st — ₱147,000 a month that must be earned before Tina takes anything home."*

**3 · Contribution margin & break-even.** Break-even sales = **fixed cost ÷ margin**. SukiMart: ₱147,000 ÷ 24% = **₱612,500/mo ≈ ₱20,400/day ≈ 170 txns** at a ₱120 basket — vs **96 txns today**. A **3.4× jump.** The margin she hits decides whether that line is reachable:

| Blended margin | Break-even/mo | Per day |
|---|---|---|
| 17.5% (today's mix) | ₱840,000 | ₱28,000 |
| 22% | ₱668,000 | ₱22,300 |
| **24%** | **₱612,500** | **₱20,400** |
| 28% (rich mix) | ₱525,000 | ₱17,500 |

🎤 *"SukiMart's fixed nut is ₱147,000. At a realistic 24% convenience margin she must sell ₱612,000 a month — 3.4× today — just to break even."*

**4 · Owner's wage vs. economic profit (the ₱25,800 illusion).** A business hasn't earned anything until it pays for **all** inputs — including the owner's labor. Pay Tina a fair **₱16,000** wage and today's true profit is **₱9,800/mo**, not ₱25,800. *(SukiMart does pay her an ₱18k salary — so even break-even SukiMart pays her more — but via ₱750k debt and ₱147k/mo of risk.)*
🎤 *"Tina thinks she earns ₱25,800. As a business paying a fair ₱16,000 wage, she earns ₱9,800. That's the number SukiMart must beat — and beat by enough to justify the risk."*

**5 · Cash view vs. P&L view (the loan trap).** Loan payment = interest (true expense, P&L) + principal (repays balance sheet, but still leaves the drawer). ₱22k/mo ≈ ₱10k interest + ₱12k principal. **We run survival on the CASH view** (all ₱22k → fixed cost ₱147k); a pure P&L would be ~₱135k. Pick one, state it.
🎤 *"We run survival on cash — all ₱22,000 of the loan leaves her drawer, so break-even is ₱147,000. On a pure P&L only the ₱10,000 interest counts. A store survives on cash, so we lead with cash."*

**6 · Sales mix.** Blended margin is a **weighted average** of what you sell. Today her bestsellers are her worst margins. The convenience format unlocks ready-to-eat (45–55%) and services (near-pure) — **that mix shift is what makes ₱612k reachable.**
🎤 *"Her margin isn't fixed — it's a weighted average. Today her bestsellers, cigarettes and load, are her worst margins. Ready-to-eat at 50% and services at near-pure margin are what move the break-even."*

**7 · Payback & ROI.** Payback = investment ÷ annual cash gain. Full SukiMart: ₱1.35M capital; **base case** ~₱27,800/mo economic profit (a real return *if base happens*, ~2yr payback); **pessimistic** ~−₱46,000/mo and ~₱389k extra liquidity needed — *no payback.* The asymmetry is the risk.
🎤 *"Base case, the full jump and our staged plan earn about the same — ₱27k a month. The difference is the downside: the full jump risks her ₱600k and can need ₱389k more in a bad year. So we earn the data first, then borrow."*

**8 · Put it together (walk it like a judge).** Real baseline ₱9,800 → must clear ₱147k fixed → ₱612k sales at 24% → ~170 txns/day → can a 'real-but-not-a-flood' corner with 6 rivals deliver that on day one? Base yes, downside ruinous → **stage it, prove it, then jump.** AI earns its place on the one quantified loss: **spoilage.**

## §6 · The recommendation & how to defend it

**Verdict: the Staged Transition (Path 2).**

**Why staged wins:**

1. **Same run-rate as the full-jump base (₱27,200 ≈ ₱27,800) — with no loan and savings safe.** You capture the convenience upside without the irreversible bet.
2. **Each upgrade self-funds the next** — services (near-pure fee margin, draws traffic) → ready-to-eat (45–55%) → chillers — so she never bets money she hasn't earned. Her ₱180k store grows toward **₱320k/mo**.
3. **It generates the demand data** to answer "can this corner carry ₱612k/mo?" — so any future loan is **gated on proof, not hope.** The data is also what the AI runs on.

**The rebuttals (drill these):**

> **"Path 3 makes more cash in year one — why not just optimize the sari-sari?"**
> *"Path 3 wins year one by about ₱77,000, but it caps Tina at ₱16,000 a month and leaves the convenience opportunity on the table. Staged reaches a ₱27,000 run-rate and builds the data and muscle to go further. We trade a one-year cash bump for a durable, higher engine and a safe path to the full store."*

> **"Aren't you just avoiding the real decision — the full build?"**
> *"No — we're sequencing it. Staged is the on-ramp. After 12 months we'll have proven demand and ₱450,000 of savings intact. If the data shows the corner carries ₱612,000 a month, Tina draws the loan THEN and builds from proof. Same destination, safer route."*

> **"Isn't staging slower — a competitor could take the corner?"**
> *"The branded store is 1.4km away and the six rivals are sari-sari, not convenience. Tina's moat is 13 years of suki trust. Staging actually defends it — she adds what customers ask for, when the data confirms demand, instead of over-building and bleeding cash if the corner under-delivers."*

## §7 · THE AI DEEP-DIVE — Smart-Prep (P4 owns this; everyone should know it)

> This is the section the judges said is worth real points and the section we lost on at PopCart. **It is built, backtested, and code-reviewed.** Reproduce any number with `./.venv/bin/python SukiMart/smartprep/run.py`.

### 7.1 What it is, in one line
**Smart-Prep** is a feature inside Tina's POS that tells her each morning **exactly how many of each ready-to-eat item to cook** — sizing every item to its own economics so she stops wasting the highest-margin food.

### 7.2 Why spoilage (why AI earns its place *here* and not everywhere)
The case's Part 6 names six places AI could help. We picked the **one with a real, quantified loss and the data to learn from**: ready-to-eat earns the **best margin (45–55%)** *and bleeds the most* — **8–15% spoilage** if demand is misjudged (Exhibit F). *"Buzzwords score nothing"* — so we costed one use, proved the payback, and mapped the rest as a data-earned roadmap (§7.9). **That discipline is the answer.**

### 7.3 The method — two real models (this is why it's NOT "just an average")
**Step A — Forecast the demand (per item, per day).** **Holt-Winters exponential smoothing** (a.k.a. triple exponential smoothing): it learns **level + trend + weekly seasonality (period = 7)** and adapts as patterns shift — plus a learned **payday multiplier** (15th/30th ±1). It is *not* a fixed multiplier; it re-weights toward recent weeks automatically.
- On short history (< 3–4 weeks) it falls back to a **seasonal baseline** (weighted average of recent same-weekdays × event factor). The model is **chosen per SKU by lowest hold-out error.**

**Step B — Model the uncertainty.** The decision needs the demand *distribution*, not just the mean:
- High-volume items (coffee, pandesal): **Normal**(μ = forecast, σ = recent forecast-error).
- Low-volume counts (rice meals, siomai): **Poisson**(λ = forecast); if variance > mean, **Negative Binomial.** *(Correct statistics for counts — and defensible.)*

**Step C — The decision: the newsvendor model** (the textbook operations-research method for perishables). Prepare the quantity where the cost of spoiling one more unit equals the cost of missing one more sale:
> **PREP `Q* = F⁻¹(CR)`**, where **`CR = Cu / (Cu + Co)`** — `Cu` = margin lost on a stock-out (underage), `Co` = cost lost on spoilage (overage).
- Normal: `Q* = μ + z·σ`, `z = Φ⁻¹(CR)`. Poisson: `Q* = poisson.ppf(CR, μ)`.

**The "smart part" — a different, costed decision per item:**
| Item | CR | Decision |
|---|---|---|
| Coffee (high margin) | **0.60** | prep **above** the mean (a missed sale costs more than a spill) |
| Pandesal (cheap to waste) | **0.40** | prep **below** the mean (spoilage costs more than a rare miss) |
*Worked example (coffee, payday): μ≈32, CR = 18/(18+12) = 0.60, z ≈ 0.25 → **Q\* ≈ 33** ("cook 33, not 34").*

🎤 **The defensibility line:** *"It's a payday-aware Holt-Winters forecast plus the newsvendor model — the standard method for perishables. A different, costed decision per item, and we backtested it. Not an average."*

### 7.4 The numbers it produces (backtested — conservative settings)
| Metric | Result |
|---|---|
| Forecast accuracy (WAPE) | **~19%** |
| Spoilage of what's prepared | **~16% (gut-feel) → ~9% (Smart-Prep)** — ~44% less waste |
| Profit protected | **~₱1,983/mo ≈ ₱24,000/yr staged → ~₱47,000/yr full** |
| In-stock (service) level | 71% → 53% at the **profit-optimal** setting — a *deliberate, tunable* trade-off |
| Incremental cost | **₱0** — a feature of the POS already in the plan (Exhibit D) |

### 7.5 We tested ML — and said so honestly
Model comparison by average WAPE: **payday-Holt-Winters 18% · Holt-Winters 19% · LightGBM 20% · seasonal baseline 21%.**
🎤 *"We tried gradient boosting — LightGBM. At ~120 days of one-store data it scored 20%, worse than Holt-Winters' 18%. ML didn't earn its complexity yet, and we said so. When she has years of data, the framework promotes it automatically."*

### 7.6 The honest trade-off — the profit-vs-availability DIAL
Smart-Prep is a **dial, not a fixed point.** At the profit-optimal setting it accepts more stock-outs on *cheap* items (over-cooking pandesal wastes more than the occasional miss). Raise the service floor and you buy availability with profit:
| Service floor | In-stock | Spoilage | Profit/yr (staged) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0.00 (profit-optimal) | 53% | 9% | **₱24,000** |
| 0.60 | 66% | 12% | ₱20,000 |
| 0.70 | 74% | 15% | ₱14,000 |
| 0.75 (≈ match today) | 79% | 17% | ₱10,000 |
🎤 *"Even matching today's availability, it still cuts waste and adds profit — because it's day-aware, not flat. Tina turns one knob to trade availability for profit."*

### 7.7 Data quality — censoring (the detail that makes it real)
On real POS data you only see what **sold**; on a sell-out, true demand was higher. In our validation, sell-outs hit **33% of item-days**, and naive "sold" **under-counts demand by ~17%** on those days. Smart-Prep **de-censors** (using the sell-out time) and recovers demand to **within ~1%** — so the forecast doesn't spiral down.
🎤 *"If you train on 'sold,' a sell-out teaches the model to forecast *less*, and it spirals. We flag sell-outs and correct for them — that's the difference between a real system and a toy."*

### 7.8 The data it needs & the tech stack (specifics for the "is it buildable?" question)
**Five small tables** — the one most teams forget is `prep_log` (it's the learning loop):
- `items` (price, cost, shelf-life, perishable flag) · `sales` (POS export, one row/line) · **`prep_log`** (prepared / sold / spoiled / sold-out flag+time — 30 seconds at close) · `calendar` (dow, payday, holiday, school-day, weather) · `forecasts` (audit trail).
- **Data needed:** ~**3–4 weeks** to learn weekly seasonality; cold-start uses category priors + Tina's gut with wide uncertainty (still beats guessing).
- **Stack:** Python 3.11 · pandas/numpy · **statsmodels** (Holt-Winters) · **scipy.stats** (Poisson/NegBin/Normal quantiles) · lightgbm (later) · SQLite/CSV storage · openpyxl/Streamlit report · matplotlib. **Runs on a phone or any laptop in milliseconds — no cloud, no GPU.**
- **Day-one reality:** the whole MVP runs in **Google Sheets** (the seasonal baseline + newsvendor are spreadsheet formulas — that's the `SukiMart_SmartPrep.xlsx` we built); production is the POS exporting CSV nightly to the engine, pushing the prep list to her phone each morning.

### 7.10 What we deliberately do NOT do (judgment, not omission)

### Strategy / the verdict
10. **Why not the full jump?** → Base run-rate (₱27,800) ≈ staged (₱27,200), but it risks her ₱600k savings + a ₱750k loan on an unproven corner; it only truly wins in the optimistic case.

14. **What's the one number to remember?** → **₱612,000** — the monthly sales SukiMart must clear to survive.

### AI / the system (own these — P4 lead, all support)
15. **Is this really AI, or just an average?** → Payday-aware **Holt-Winters + the newsvendor model** — both standard, both backtested. A costed decision *per item*, not an average.
16. **What forecasting model, exactly?** → Holt-Winters (triple exponential smoothing, weekly seasonality period 7) for the mean; Poisson/Negative-Binomial or Normal for the distribution; **newsvendor** for the quantity.
17. **What data does it need?** → Daily sales + a `prep_log` (prepared/sold/spoiled/sold-out) + a calendar (payday/holiday/weather). ~3–4 weeks to learn the weekly pattern; works from day one on priors.
18. **Did you try machine learning?** → Yes — LightGBM scored **20% WAPE, worse than Holt-Winters' 18%** at ~120 days of one-store data. We deploy the simpler model and said so.
19. **How much does it save, and at what cost?** → **~₱24k/yr** staged, **~₱47k** at full scale, **₱0 incremental** — it's a feature of the POS she's buying anyway.
20. **Why does your in-stock drop to 53%?** → That's the *profit-max* setting, deliberately on cheap items where over-cooking wastes more than a rare miss. It's a **dial** — match today's ~79% availability and it still cuts waste and adds profit.
21. **Why only ₱24k when your model shows more?** → Our top-down ceiling is ~₱67k/yr, but we cite the **backtested, net ₱24k** — net of sales given up and valued at cost. We under-promise.
22. **Is AI overkill for a sari-sari?** → The forecasting runs in a **spreadsheet** in milliseconds and Tina reads one number per item. It's affordable and explainable — that's the point.
23. **Why only spoilage — is that all AI can do here?** → Spoilage is where AI pays *today*. It's **one engine**: it extends to reorder across 900 SKUs, utang scoring, basket lift, and staffing-to-traffic — exactly the openings the case lists — switching on as the data arrives.
24. **Can Tina actually run it?** → The system recommends; **Tina decides.** She reads the morning list; the engine is built once by a dev and runs nightly.
25. **What did you choose NOT to build, and why?** → Surge-pricing and customer surveillance — they'd erode the suki trust that is her real asset.
26. **How does the AI handle a sell-out?** → It flags it and de-censors (true demand ≥ sold); otherwise the forecast would spiral down. Naive data under-counts by ~17%; we recover to ~1%.

### Curveballs
27. **What if she can't collect the prep_log discipline?** → Day-one it's a 30-second tally; the POS automates it later. Without it she can't measure spoilage at all — so it's the first habit we build (M1–2).
28. **What if the cooperative loan terms change?** → Doesn't affect Year 1 — we don't draw the loan. The M9 gate re-prices the decision with whatever terms exist then.
29. **Isn't ₱9,800 unfair — she's happy with ₱25,800 cash?** → For her *household* cash flow, ₱25,800 is real. But to compare against SukiMart (which pays her a salary), we must value her labor — otherwise we'd compare a job-plus-business to a business and mislead her.

31. **If you had to cut one thing from the plan, what?** → The chillers (M6–8) before services/ready-to-eat — services draw traffic and ready-to-eat carries the margin + the AI story; chillers are the most deferrable.

# SukiMart — Business Crashcourse
### The 8 ideas that decide this case, in plain language, with Tina's real numbers

> **Who this is for:** a team with little formal business background that needs to *understand the problem, make the call, and defend every number on stage.* Read once top-to-bottom. Then drill the **🎤 On stage** line under each idea — that's the sentence that wins (or loses) the Q&A.
>
> **The whole case in one sentence:** Tina feels rich (₱25,800/month) running an informal store where her biggest costs are invisible; the question is whether a formal SukiMart — where every cost becomes a real bill — can earn *more* than that, or just make her *busier and poorer.*

---

## 0 · The one-page mental model
A business has three layers. Most owners (and most losing teams) only see the top one.
1. **Markup** — how much you add on top of cost. *What Tina watches.*
2. **Margin & gross profit** — what's left after the cost of the goods. *Lower than markup, always.*
3. **Net profit** — what's left after *all* the other costs (rent, staff, electricity, loan, and a fair wage for the owner). *This is the only number that actually feeds a family safely.*

SukiMart is dangerous because it takes costs that are **invisible** in a sari-sari (rent, labor, electricity) and turns them into **hard monthly bills**. The same markup now has to feed all of them *before* Tina earns a peso.

---

## 1 · Markup vs. Margin — the two words that decide this case
- **Markup** = profit measured against **cost**. Buy at ₱100, sell at ₱120 → markup is **20%** (the ₱20 vs the ₱100 cost).
- **Margin** = the same profit measured against the **selling price**. ₱20 ÷ ₱120 = **16.7%**.
- **They are never the same. Margin is always smaller.** Convert: `margin = markup ÷ (1 + markup)`.

**Tina's reality (Exhibit A/B):** She *says* her markup is 20%. But her books show COGS is 82.5% of sales, so her **blended gross margin is 17.5%.** Her starting number is already softer than she believes.

🎤 **On stage:** *"Tina's '20%' is a markup on cost. Measured against her selling price — which is how you actually run a P&L — it's a 17.5% margin. Every peso of profit in this case is calculated on that 17.5%, not 20%."*

---

## 2 · Fixed vs. Variable costs — why sari-sari survives where formal retail dies
- **Variable cost** moves with each sale. Mainly the **cost of the goods** (COGS). Sell nothing → pay nothing.
- **Fixed cost** arrives every month whether you sell or not: rent, staff salaries, the loan, insurance, permits.

**The trick this case is built on:** in Tina's sari-sari, her fixed costs are **hidden** —
- Rent = ₱0 (the store is her house)
- Her labor = ₱0 (she pays herself nothing)
- Electricity = buried in the household bill (~₱2,800)
So a 17.5% margin really does land in her pocket. **The moment she formalizes, those invisible costs become ₱147,000/month of hard, fixed bills** (Exhibit D). That is why "formalizing a business can quietly destroy its margin."

🎤 **On stage:** *"A sari-sari hides its three biggest costs — rent, labor, electricity. SukiMart turns all three into bills due on the 1st. That's ₱147,000 a month that must be earned before Tina takes anything home."*

---

## 3 · Contribution margin & break-even — the most important tool you have
- **Contribution margin** = the share of each peso of sales left *after variable cost* to "contribute" toward fixed costs. For this store it's ≈ the gross margin (COGS is the main variable cost).
- **Break-even** = the sales level where total contribution exactly equals fixed costs. Below it you lose money; above it you profit.
> **Break-even sales = Fixed costs ÷ Contribution margin**

**SukiMart break-even (cash view):** ₱147,000 ÷ 24% margin = **₱612,500/month ≈ ₱20,400/day.**
The store today does **₱180,000/month (₱6,000/day)**. Break-even is a **3.4× jump.** In transactions: ~**170/day at a ₱120 basket**, versus **96/day today.**

| Blended margin | Break-even sales/month | Per day |
|---|---|---|
| 17.5% (today's mix) | ₱840,000 | ₱28,000 |
| 22% | ₱668,000 | ₱22,300 |
| 24% | ₱612,500 | ₱20,400 |
| 28% (rich mix) | ₱525,000 | ₱17,500 |

🎤 **On stage:** *"SukiMart's fixed nut is ₱147,000. At a realistic 24% convenience margin, she must sell ₱612,000 a month just to break even — 3.4 times today. The margin she achieves decides whether that line is reachable."*

---

## 4 · Owner's wage vs. economic profit — the ₱25,800 illusion
A business hasn't really "earned" anything until it has paid for **all** its inputs — *including the owner's own labor.*
- Tina believes she earns **₱25,800/month** (cash left after small costs).
- But she works ~16 hours/day for **₱0 salary.** A fair replacement wage is **₱16,000** (Exhibit A memo).
- Subtract it: her store's **true economic profit is only ₱9,800/month.**

This is the real baseline SukiMart must beat. SukiMart *does* pay Tina an ₱18,000 salary (Exhibit D) — so even break-even SukiMart pays her more than her true economic profit today. But it does so by taking on ₱750k of debt and ₱147k/month of risk.

🎤 **On stage:** *"Tina thinks she earns ₱25,800. As a business, paying herself a fair ₱16,000 wage, she earns ₱9,800. That ₱9,800 — not ₱25,800 — is the number SukiMart has to beat, and beat by enough to justify the risk."*

---

## 5 · Cash view vs. P&L view — the loan trap your mentor will probe
A loan payment has two parts:
- **Interest** — a true *expense* (the cost of borrowing). Shows on the P&L.
- **Principal** — *not* an expense; it repays what you owe (balance sheet). But it **still leaves the cash drawer.**

Tina's ₱22,000/month loan payment ≈ **₱10,000 interest + ₱12,000 principal** (₱750k @ 18% over 48 months).
- **For survival, use the CASH view:** all ₱22,000 must be earned every month → fixed cost ₱147,000.
- **For profitability (accounting), use the P&L view:** only the ₱10,000 interest counts → fixed cost ~₱135,000.
- **Rule: pick one view, state it, and be consistent.** The case explicitly warns about this.

🎤 **On stage:** *"We run the survival case on cash — all ₱22,000 of the loan leaves her drawer, so break-even is ₱147,000. On a pure P&L, only the ₱10,000 interest is an expense, so it's ~₱135,000. We lead with the cash view, because a store survives on cash, not on accounting."*

---

## 6 · Sales mix — the hidden lever that moves the margin
Total margin isn't one number you set; it's the **weighted average of what you sell.** Change the mix, change the margin.

**Tina's trap today (Exhibit B):** her *highest-volume* categories earn the *thinnest* margins.
| Sells a lot of (thin) | Margin | Sells little of (fat) | Margin |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cigarettes (14% of sales) | 9% | Ice & misc (4%) | 30% |
| Mobile load (8%) | 8% | Sachet tingi (15%) | 28% |
| Rice per gatang (9%) | 10% | Bread (6%) | 25% |

**The convenience upgrade (Exhibit F)** opens *new* high-margin lines she can't sell as a sari-sari:
- **Ready-to-eat** (rice meals, siomai, hot coffee): **45–55%** — but spoils fast (this is where AI earns its place).
- **Services** (bills payment, e-wallet cash-in/out, padala, load): **fee-based, almost pure margin**, and a powerful reason to walk in.
- **Chilled beverages** 18–22%, **snacks** 25–30%.

So the path to a 24–28% blended margin (which lowers break-even) is **shifting the mix toward ready-to-eat + services**, not selling more cigarettes.

🎤 **On stage:** *"Her blended margin isn't fixed — it's a weighted average. Today her bestsellers (cigarettes, load) are her worst margins. The convenience format lets her add ready-to-eat at 50% and services at near-pure margin. That mix shift is what makes the break-even reachable — or not."*

---

## 7 · Payback & ROI — is the investment worth it?
- **Payback period** = how long until the profit earned repays the money put in. `Payback = investment ÷ annual net cash gain.`
- **ROI (return on investment)** = annual gain ÷ money invested, as a %.

**SukiMart:** total capital **₱1,350,000** (₱600k her savings + ₱750k loan).
- *Base case* throws off a **+₱25,800/month surplus** *after* paying her an ₱18k salary and the full loan. On a fair-wage basis (₱16k) that's **₱27,800/month economic profit** — the apples-to-apples number we use to compare paths. Either way: a real return *if the base case happens.*
- *Pessimistic case* is a **−₱48,000/month surplus (≈ −₱46,000 economic profit)**: no payback. Her ₱600k is already sunk as capital, so the model shows she'd need **~₱389,000 of extra liquidity in Year 1** to keep going, with the loan still due. **That asymmetry is the risk.**
- *Honest caveat:* assets keep residual value and the loan principal amortizes, so it isn't "₱600k vaporized" — it's ₱600k **at risk** plus a real cash-shortfall in a weak year.

🎤 **On stage:** *"Base case, SukiMart and the staged plan earn almost the same — about ₱27k a month. The difference is the downside: the full jump puts her ₱600k at risk and can need ₱389k more cash in a bad year. So we earn the data first, then borrow."*

---

## 8 · Putting it together — how to *think* about Tina's decision
Walk the logic the way a judge will:
1. **What's the real baseline?** ₱9,800/month economic profit (not ₱25,800).
2. **What must SukiMart clear?** ₱147,000/month fixed (cash view) → ₱612k sales at 24% → ~170 txns/day.
3. **Can the location deliver it?** 1,800 passersby, 96 txns today, "real but not a flood," 6 rival sari-sari. The base case is *possible*, the downside is *ruinous*.
4. **So which path?** The one that **captures the upside without betting the savings on an unproven number.** That points to the **staged transition** — add ready-to-eat + services + a chiller (low capex, high margin, self-funding), *measure* whether transactions and basket actually climb toward 170, and take the ₱750k loan only once the data says the location can carry it.
5. **Where does AI earn its place?** The one spot with a real, quantified loss: **perishable spoilage forecasting** (waste of what's prepped ~16% → ~9%): **~₱24k/yr at staged scale, ~₱47k/yr at full SukiMart** — a feature of the POS already in the plan. One use. Costed. Backtested (payday-Holt-Winters + newsvendor; it even beat LightGBM).

🎤 **On stage (the whole thesis):** *"Tina's real profit is ₱9,800, not ₱25,800. SukiMart must earn ₱147,000 a month before she's paid — a 3.4× jump at a location that's real but not a flood. So we don't bet her savings on day one. We add the high-margin pieces that fund themselves, prove the demand with data, and let AI kill the spoilage that would otherwise sink the highest-margin shelf — then jump when the numbers, not hope, say go."*

## Quick glossary
| Term | Plain meaning | Tina's number |
|---|---|---|
| Markup | profit ÷ **cost** | ~21% (she says 20%) |
| Gross margin | profit ÷ **price** | **17.5%** |
| Gross profit | sales − cost of goods | ₱31,500/mo today |
| Variable cost | moves with sales (COGS) | 82.5% of sales |
| Fixed cost | due every month regardless | ₱147,000/mo (SukiMart) |
| Contribution margin | sales − variable, per peso | ≈ gross margin |
| Break-even | sales where contribution = fixed | ₱612,500/mo @24% |
| Economic profit | profit after a fair owner wage | **₱9,800/mo today** |
| Payback | time to repay the investment | ~2 yrs (base case) |

## 🎤 15 likely judge questions — and the one-line answer
1. **Is 20% markup or margin?** Markup on cost; margin is 17.5% on price.
2. **What does Tina really earn?** ₱9,800/mo economic profit, not ₱25,800 (after a ₱16k fair wage).
3. **What's the break-even?** ₱147k fixed ÷ ~24% = ₱612k/mo ≈ ₱20,400/day.
4. **How big a jump is that?** 3.4× today's ₱180k; ~170 txns/day vs 96.
5. **Can the location deliver?** Base case yes; downside runs ≈ −₱46k/mo economic profit — too risky to bet day one.
6. **Cash or P&L view?** Cash for survival (₱147k); P&L is ~₱135k. We're consistent on cash.
7. **Why not the full jump?** It bets her ₱600k savings + ₱750k debt on an unproven sales number.
8. **What lifts the margin?** Mix shift to ready-to-eat (45–55%) + services (near-pure), not cigarettes (9%).
9. **Where does AI earn its place?** Perishable spoilage forecasting — ~₱24k/yr at staged scale, ~₱47k/yr at full SukiMart.
10. **Is that real AI or just averages?** Demand forecasting on time/day/season patterns (Exhibit G) — costed in the POS.
11. **Why staged, not stay sari-sari?** Staged captures convenience upside while staying low-risk; pure sari-sari caps the ceiling.
12. **What's the loan cost?** ₱750k @18%/48mo ≈ ₱22k/mo (~₱10k interest, ₱12k principal).
13. **Biggest risk?** Sales come in at the low end with the loan due → ≈ −₱46k/mo and ~₱389k extra liquidity needed. Mitigation: prove demand before borrowing.
14. **Spoilage risk?** Perishables bleed 8–15% if misjudged — the AI forecast is exactly the control.
15. **What's the one number to remember?** ₱612,000 — the monthly sales SukiMart must clear to survive.

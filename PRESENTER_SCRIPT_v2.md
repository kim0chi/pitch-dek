# PopCart PH — 7-Minute Pitch Script (v2 deck)
### UCLM · Team SiXeven · "Fix the Engine Before You Grow the Body"

> **Matched slide-by-slide to `UCLM_SiXeven_LAMBO2026.pptx` (15 content slides).** Runs ~7:00 at ~140 wpm with pauses. `║` = hand-off point for a 4-person team. **Bold** = numbers to land slowly. `[ ]` = stage direction. 🛡️ = a line written to *pre-empt a question your mentor flagged* — say it out loud so the judges never get to ask.
>
> **Delivery rule:** talk to one founder, not a panel — "your shelves," "your margin." Slow down on every peso figure.

---

### COVER — *PopCart makes ₱120M a year* · (0:00–0:20)
> "Judges, imagine you own PopCart. Last year: **₱120 million** in sales, profitable, going viral. Life is good.
> [pause] And yet you're quietly bleeding away most of your profit — and you can't see where. In seven minutes, we'll show you the leak, and the cheapest way to stop it."

### 1/15 — The Obvious Trap · (0:20–1:05)  ║ *(Presenter 1)*
> "We started where most people would. Shipping to Visayas and Mindanao is brutal — **₱155 to ₱185 an order** (Exhibit B). The obvious fix? Build a hub in Cebu. So before recommending anything, we ran the numbers on it.
> [pause] We found a trap. A Cebu hub costs about **₱9 million a year to run**, but saves only **₱2.4 million** in shipping. 🛡️ Even after we add damage savings *and* credit it the case's growth, the base case still **loses about ₱4 million a year** — it would need roughly **40% regional growth** to break even, and the case only assumes 15 to 30%.
> On a **9% margin**, that's a gamble you can't afford. So we asked: if not the hub — what's actually bleeding the money?"

### 2/15 — The Real Villain · (1:05–1:45)
> "It isn't logistics. It's **empty shelves.**
> Your best-seller, freeze-dried candy, sells 300 a day — but it's **out of stock 11 days a month.** One day in three, that demand just walks out the door.
> 🛡️ Add it up across your fast-movers and that's a **₱7.35 million gross-profit leak** — equal to about **68% of your entire net profit** (Exhibit C, at the Exhibit A margin). To be precise: that's gross profit lost, sized against net profit — and unlike the hub, the fix costs almost nothing."

### 3/15 — The Demand Is Already There · (1:45–2:10)  ║ *(Presenter 2)*
> "And the demand is real. Candy moves 300 a day *even while out of stock.* The customers are there — PopCart just can't keep the shelf full or get it to them.
> Central Visayas and Davao are **43% of orders** (Exhibit B) — the slowest and most damaged regions. That's where expansion *eventually* lives. But you earn that by capturing the demand you already lose first."

### 4/15 — The Recommendation · (2:10–2:35)
> "So here's our call, in one picture: **you don't buy a new car when the gas tank has a hole in it.**
> **Step one — fix the engine:** stop the ₱7.35M leak with a lightweight data system. **Step two — grow the body:** once margins stabilise, expand asset-light, never buying expensive real estate. One move at a time, in sequence — never everything at once."

### 5/15 — The Options, Side by Side · (2:35–3:10)  ║ *(Presenter 3)*
> "We didn't just assert that — we modeled every option, worst case to best.
> The Cebu hub ranges from **minus ₱6.9 million** to plus ₱1 million. The kiosk, minus ₱2.5 to plus ₱3. But fixing operations? Worst case **minus ₱0.5M**, base case **positive** — by far the smallest downside.
> 🛡️ And we were tough on ourselves: we credited the hub's growth *generously* and still it loses. On a thin margin, you protect the downside first."

### 6/15 — Reorder Before You Run Out · (3:10–3:45)
> "So how do we stop the leak? Not by working harder — by reordering *earlier.*
> Today the founders reorder on gut feel. But with **7-to-30-day lead times**, by the time a shelf looks empty, it's already too late.
> 🛡️ The rule is simple: reorder when stock drops to **daily sales × lead time, plus a safety-stock buffer** — not when it hits zero. For ramen, with its 30-day lead, that reorder point is about **7,200 units.** These are calculated levels, not guesses."

### 7/15 — The Warning System · (3:45–4:30)  ║ *(Presenter 4)*
> "We built that into a lightweight warning system — think a **low-fuel light for snacks.** Not a bloated ERP; a dashboard a 3-person team can run.
> It does three things: **pull** the order data in, **forecast** each product's run-out date, and **alert** the founder before stock hits the safety line. The system recommends; a human decides.
> 🛡️ Two honest points. First, the forecast is **simple, transparent math** — weighted 7-, 14-, and 28-day averages plus a safety-stock formula — *not* a black-box AI; that's a feature, because the team can actually trust and tune it. Second, TikTok has **no free native connector** to Sheets, so we budget a low-cost connector or a scheduled daily export. We costed that in."

### 8/15 — Don't Build on Rented Land · (4:30–5:00)
> "One more risk worth naming: **90% of sales sit on TikTok Shop.** One algorithm change could tank the business overnight. That's not a business — it's a tenancy.
> So in months four to six we add a **low-code webstore.** 🛡️ We're not building software — it's an off-the-shelf store. We know most volume stays on TikTok, the same way McDo leans on Grab. The webstore isn't a replacement — it's **insurance, better margin per order, and we finally own the customer data.**"

### 9/15 — Take a Grab, Don't Buy the Car · (5:00–5:20)  ║ *(Presenter 1)*
> "And when we *do* test Cebu, we won't buy a warehouse. We use a third-party logistics partner — an outside warehouse that ships for a per-order fee. It's the difference between **taking a Grab and buying a car**: pay only when you ride, and pilot before you commit to a fixed cost."

### 10/15 — The Honest Money · (5:20–5:55)
> "Now the money — honestly. The ₱7.35M is the *leak*, not the prize. We only recover a fraction.
> A ₱180 candy keeps about **₱54** after cost of goods and shipping — so we value recovery on **take-home pay, not gross.** Run the chain — recover ~80% of the lost days at a 30% margin, minus ₱0.9M a year to operate — and you land on about **plus ₱0.6 million a year**, in a range of ₱0.1 to 1.6 million. Setup is ₱1.4M. We'd rather under-promise."

### 11/15 — Payback Without Pretending · (5:55–6:25)  ║ *(Presenter 2)*
> "Which gives a payback of about **31 months** at our cautious margin. Is that slow? We say so honestly — the case allows it. Year one is an **investment year, minus ₱1.18 million cash**; net-positive every year after.
> 🛡️ On the chart, the worst-case bar is greyed and *not to scale* on purpose — at a 20% margin payback is effectively 'never,' and we won't pretend otherwise. Compare that to the hub, which never pays back at all."

### 12/15 — The 12-Month Roadmap · (6:25–6:45)
> "The plan sequences deliberately: build the habit first, *then* test expansion. Baseline and pilot the warning system, stabilise, launch the webstore — then **Month 7, a hard decision gate.**
> 🛡️ We don't pile everything into one quarter — the gate is the buffer. The 3PL pilot only happens *after* stabilisation, and only if the unit economics clear it."

### 13/15 — Risks & Stop-Rules · (6:45–6:55)  ║ *(Presenter 3)*
> "And we tried to break our own plan. Every risk has a mitigation *and* a stop-rule — a pre-agreed line where we cut scope. If the economics turn negative, we stop. That's discipline, not optimism."

### 14/15 + 15/15 — The Payoff & Close · (6:55–7:15)  ║ *(Presenter 4)*
> "The result: stockouts roughly halved, inventory live every two hours, margin climbing toward 10% — without betting the company.
> So here's the bottom line. PopCart doesn't need to gamble on the newest hub to grow. It needs to **stop the leak, then earn the growth.**
> Because success isn't found in the newest hub — it's in **the precision of the shelf.** [pause] Thank you. We'd love your questions."
